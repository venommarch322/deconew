Spiced Shakshuka

⏱ Prep: 10 min 🔥 Cook: 20 min ⏳ Total: 30 min 🍽 Serves: 4
280Calories
18gProtein
16gFat
18gCarbs

Shakshuka is one of the most beloved breakfast and brunch dishes across the entire Middle East and North Africa, and once you make it at home for the first time, you will instantly understand why it has captured the hearts and appetites of food lovers around the world. At its essence, shakshuka is a stunningly simple concept — eggs are gently poached in a richly spiced, slow-simmered tomato and pepper sauce until the whites are just set and the yolks remain gloriously runny. The dish is served directly in the skillet, still bubbling and fragrant, alongside thick slices of warm crusty bread that you tear apart and use to scoop up the saucy, egg-rich goodness. It is communal, it is rustic, it is deeply comforting, and it is absolutely one of the most satisfying meals you can make in under thirty minutes.

What elevates shakshuka from a basic eggs-in-tomato-sauce dish to something truly spectacular is the careful layering of spices and aromatics. Cumin adds its distinctive earthy warmth, smoked paprika contributes a gorgeous depth and color, chili flakes provide a gentle background heat that builds gradually with each bite, and fresh garlic infuses the entire sauce with pungent, aromatic intensity. The onions and bell peppers are cooked slowly until they soften completely and meld into the tomato sauce, creating a thick, jammy base that cradles the eggs beautifully and soaks into bread like nothing else. A touch of sugar balances the natural acidity of the tomatoes and rounds out the flavors into perfect harmony.

The beauty of shakshuka lies not only in its extraordinary flavor but also in its remarkable versatility and simplicity. It requires just one skillet, a handful of pantry staples, and roughly thirty minutes of your time — making it an ideal choice for lazy weekend brunches, quick weeknight dinners when you need something satisfying without the effort, or even an impressive dish to serve to guests who will be astonished by how something so simple can taste so complex and restaurant-worthy. Every element of this recipe is designed to build maximum flavor with minimum effort, and the result is a dish that punches far above its weight in terms of impressiveness versus the actual work involved.

Ingredients

  • 2 tablespoons extra-virgin olive oil
  • 1 large yellow onion, finely diced
  • 1 large red bell pepper, finely diced
  • 4 garlic cloves, thinly sliced
  • 1 teaspoon ground cumin
  • 1 teaspoon smoked paprika
  • 1/2 teaspoon red chili flakes (adjust to heat preference)
  • 1 can (28 oz) crushed San Marzano tomatoes
  • 1 teaspoon sugar
  • Salt and freshly ground black pepper
  • 6 large eggs
  • Fresh cilantro or flat-leaf parsley for garnish
  • Crumbled feta cheese (optional but highly recommended)
  • Warm crusty bread, pita, or challah for serving

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Build the aromatic base: Heat the olive oil in a large, deep skillet — cast iron is ideal for even heat distribution and that beautiful, rustic presentation at the table — over medium heat. Add the diced onion and red bell pepper and cook for seven to eight minutes, stirring occasionally, until they are completely soft, translucent, and just beginning to caramelize at the edges. The slow cooking of these vegetables is essential — it draws out their natural sweetness and creates a deeply flavored foundation that elevates the entire dish. Add the sliced garlic, cumin, smoked paprika, and chili flakes, stirring constantly for sixty seconds until the spices bloom and become explosively fragrant. The kitchen will fill with an extraordinary aroma that announces something wonderful is happening.
  2. Simmer the sauce: Pour in the can of crushed tomatoes and stir to combine everything thoroughly with the aromatic vegetable base. Add the sugar, a generous teaspoon of salt, and several cracks of black pepper. Bring the sauce to a gentle simmer and let it cook undisturbed for ten to twelve minutes, stirring occasionally, until it reduces and thickens into a rich, jammy consistency. The sauce should be thick enough that when you drag a spoon through it, the trail holds for a moment before slowly filling back in. Taste at this point and adjust the seasoning — you may want more salt, more chili for heat, or a pinch more sugar to balance the tomato acidity. Getting this sauce perfectly seasoned before adding the eggs is crucial because the eggs cook quickly and you will not have time to adjust afterward.
  3. Create wells and add eggs: Using the back of a large spoon, create six evenly spaced wells or indentations in the surface of the thickened sauce. These wells will cradle the eggs and help them cook evenly. Crack one egg carefully into each well, being careful not to break the yolks. Season the tops of the eggs with a tiny pinch of salt and pepper.
  4. Poach the eggs: Reduce the heat to medium-low, cover the skillet with a tight-fitting lid, and cook for five to eight minutes depending on how runny you like your yolks. For gloriously runny yolks that burst when you break them with bread — which is the traditional and most popular way to serve shakshuka — check at five minutes. The whites should be fully set and opaque while the yolks remain soft, jiggly, and liquid when you gently press them with a fingertip. If you prefer firmer yolks, cook for the full eight minutes with the lid on. Remember that the eggs will continue cooking slightly from the residual heat of the sauce even after you remove the lid.
  5. Garnish and serve: Remove the lid and immediately scatter generous handfuls of freshly chopped cilantro or parsley over the top. Crumble feta cheese generously across the entire surface — the salty, tangy feta is an incredible complement to the smoky, spiced tomato sauce and the rich, runny egg yolks. Serve the shakshuka immediately, directly in the skillet while it is still gently bubbling, with plenty of warm crusty bread, thick-cut toast, or pita bread on the side for tearing and scooping. Eating shakshuka is meant to be communal and interactive — everyone gathers around the pan, tears bread, and scoops up sauce and egg together in the most satisfyingly messy, delicious way imaginable.
🫒 Olive & Thyme Tips:
• Shakshuka is infinitely customizable — add crumbled merguez sausage, sautéed spinach or kale, roasted eggplant, or a handful of chickpeas for a more substantial version.
• The quality of your canned tomatoes makes an enormous difference. San Marzano or other high-quality Italian crushed tomatoes produce a sweeter, less acidic, more flavorful sauce.
• For a North African variation, add half a teaspoon of harissa paste to the sauce for a smoky, complex heat that is absolutely magnificent.
• Leftover sauce (without the eggs) keeps in the refrigerator for five days and freezes for three months — simply reheat and poach fresh eggs when ready to serve.
• Shakshuka is traditionally served for breakfast or brunch, but it makes an equally wonderful quick dinner — do not limit this magnificent dish to mornings only.
