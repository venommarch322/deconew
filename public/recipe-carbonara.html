Authentic Spaghetti Carbonara

⏱ Prep: 10 min🔥 Cook: 15 min⏳ Total: 25 min🍽 Serves: 4
560Calories
22gProtein
26gFat
52gCarbs

Spaghetti alla carbonara is one of the four sacred pasta dishes of Rome — along with cacio e pepe, amatriciana, and gricia — and it is perhaps the most misunderstood and incorrectly prepared Italian recipe in the entire world outside of Italy. True Roman carbonara contains absolutely no cream whatsoever. None. Zero. The silky, velvety, golden sauce that coats each strand of spaghetti in luxurious richness is created entirely from egg yolks, finely grated Pecorino Romano cheese, and the rendered fat from guanciale — cured pork jowl that is the traditional meat used in this dish. The heat of the freshly cooked pasta gently cooks the egg-and-cheese mixture into a smooth, emulsified sauce that clings to the noodles like liquid gold, creating a dish of breathtaking simplicity and extraordinary depth of flavor.

The technique for making proper carbonara is deceptively simple but requires precise timing and temperature control to achieve that perfect, silky consistency without accidentally scrambling the eggs into dry, rubbery curds clinging to your pasta — which is, unfortunately, the fate of many home cooks' first attempts. The key is removing the pan completely from the heat before adding the egg mixture, and using the residual heat of the pasta and rendered fat to gently, gradually cook the eggs into a smooth, creamy sauce. The starchy pasta cooking water is your secret weapon here — it helps emulsify the sauce and allows you to adjust the consistency from thick and clingy to silky and flowing. Mastering carbonara is a rite of passage for any serious home cook, and this recipe walks you through every critical detail.

With just five core ingredients and twenty-five minutes of your time, you can create a restaurant-quality carbonara that would make a Roman nonna proud. It is one of those rare dishes where the whole is dramatically greater than the sum of its parts — simple ingredients elevated by proper technique into something that feels impossibly luxurious and complex. The crispy, salty, deeply savory guanciale provides bursts of concentrated pork flavor, the Pecorino adds sharp, tangy saltiness, the egg yolks bring richness and body, and the black pepper contributes a gentle, warming heat that ties everything together in perfect balance.

Ingredients

  • 400g (14 oz) spaghetti or rigatoni
  • 200g (7 oz) guanciale — or pancetta if unavailable (never bacon)
  • 4 large egg yolks + 2 whole eggs
  • 1.5 cups finely grated Pecorino Romano (or a 50/50 mix of Pecorino and Parmigiano-Reggiano)
  • Generous freshly cracked black pepper
  • Salt for pasta water only

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Prepare the egg mixture: In a medium bowl, whisk together the four egg yolks, two whole eggs, and most of the grated Pecorino Romano — reserve a generous handful for garnishing at the end. Add an extremely generous amount of freshly cracked black pepper — more than you think you need. Carbonara should have a noticeable peppery warmth that balances the rich, fatty sauce. The pepper is not a background player here; it is a starring ingredient. Whisk the mixture until completely smooth, thick, and uniform. Set it aside while you prepare the rest of the dish.
  2. Cook the guanciale: Cut the guanciale into strips or small cubes, depending on your preference. Place them in a large, cold skillet — starting in a cold pan allows the fat to render slowly and evenly, producing the crispiest, most flavorful result. Turn the heat to medium and cook for eight to ten minutes, stirring occasionally, until the guanciale is deeply golden brown and crispy on the edges while remaining slightly chewy in the center. The rendered fat in the pan is pure liquid gold — it becomes the primary fat in the sauce. Remove the skillet from the heat but leave the guanciale and all of its rendered fat in the pan. This fat will help emulsify the egg mixture into the silky sauce that makes carbonara so distinctive and special.
  3. Cook the pasta: Meanwhile, bring a large pot of generously salted water to a rolling boil. Cook the spaghetti according to package directions until it is one minute short of al dente — it will finish cooking in the pan with the sauce, absorbing additional flavor and reaching perfect doneness. Before draining, scoop out at least two full cups of the starchy pasta cooking water. This cloudy, starch-laden liquid is absolutely essential for building the emulsified sauce — the dissolved starch acts as a natural binding agent that helps the egg and cheese mixture transform into a smooth, cohesive sauce instead of a clumpy, broken mess. Drain the pasta.
  4. Combine pasta and guanciale: Add the hot, drained pasta directly into the skillet with the guanciale and rendered fat. The skillet should be OFF the heat at this point — this is critical and non-negotiable. Toss the pasta vigorously with tongs for thirty seconds to coat every strand in the flavorful rendered fat and to incorporate the crispy guanciale throughout.
  5. Add the egg mixture: With the skillet still completely off the heat, pour the egg-and-cheese mixture over the hot pasta. Toss vigorously and constantly with tongs, lifting and turning the spaghetti for a full sixty seconds. The residual heat from the pasta and the fat will gently cook the egg mixture into a silky, emulsified sauce that clings to every strand without scrambling. If the sauce seems too thick, add the reserved pasta water one tablespoon at a time, tossing after each addition, until you achieve a smooth, glossy, flowing consistency that is neither watery nor gluey. The perfect carbonara sauce should coat the back of a spoon and flow like thick cream when you lift a forkful of pasta from the bowl.
  6. Serve immediately: Transfer to warm bowls without delay — carbonara waits for no one and will continue to thicken as it sits. Top each portion with the reserved Pecorino Romano and another generous crack of black pepper. Serve immediately while the sauce is still silky, glossy, and at its absolute peak of perfection.
🫒 Olive & Thyme Tips:
• The single most critical rule: NEVER add the egg mixture while the pan is on the heat. The residual heat alone is sufficient. Direct heat will scramble the eggs instantly and ruin the dish.
• Guanciale is the traditional and ideal meat — it has a richer, more complex flavor than pancetta. Find it at Italian delis, specialty grocery stores, or order online. If absolutely unavailable, use pancetta — but never bacon, which has a smoked flavor that does not belong in carbonara.
• Use Pecorino Romano, not generic "Pecorino." True Romano has a sharp, salty, tangy flavor that is irreplaceable in this recipe.
• Save more pasta water than you think — you can always discard what you do not use, but you cannot get it back once you have drained the pot.
• Warm your serving bowls in the oven at 200°F for five minutes before plating — this keeps the carbonara at the perfect temperature and prevents the sauce from seizing up on cold plates.
